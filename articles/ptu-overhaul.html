Making a Pokemon RPG Less "Crunchy"

Where did this come from?

So me and my friends wanted to play a Pokemon Tabletop RPG, and we chose Pokemon Tabletop United (PTU for short). This game is fun and crunchy. And my friends didn’t especially like crunching numbers during sessions, so the game ended up losing popularity in our playgroup.

Crunchy
This is by no means a bad thing, in my opinion it speaks of the nature of the game. It also sort of works like a scale, the more and more complex math you need to do during your playing session, the crunchier the game becomes. If in a given system,I need to add up four different situational bonuses to roll a hit, then that system is crunchier than one where I need to roll a single die with just one static bonus.


As an example, Powered By the Apocalypse games have little crunch. Shadowrun is crunchy. Dungeons & Dragons 3.5e is crunchier than 5th Edition and Pokemon Tabletop United is crunchier than the version I wrote.


We were having fun while playing, so in an attempt to recover the group's attention I developed my own PTU version. It all started with suggestions I had and were piling up in my head. Those ended up as whole cuts and changes to the original game and in the following sections I will go over the most relevant, detailing my thought process behind my decisions.


Divided by 5

This was the kickoff change, the one that set out the chain reaction.

The game is about Pokemon, and the pokemon in those games level up from level 1 to 100. This means that if I were to adapt Pokemon to a d20 system, it would make sense to develop a d20 system and then multiply it by 5, then everything looks one-hundred-ish. This may or may not be the way the developers designed the game, it's just a pattern I definitely noticed.

Then the general smallest bonus unit in the game is a +5. There are some exceptions, but this is a rule, and the other bonuses are generally multiples of five.

Implementing this change was fairly straightforward. Just divide most of the numerical values by 5 and we get the simplified bonus.

In the same vein, I simplified the damage bases. Pokemon games have a lot of attacks, called Moves, PTU has all of those moves as wells but, beside the dice rolled for a move, there is a number which can be referenced in a table where it tells you how many dice you would roll when using that Move. Why? Well because there are bonuses and penalties to the damage base of the move. So you get a +1, go to the chart, get the dice you need to roll and then calculate your additional situational bonuses.

To reduce the crunchiness, which was my aim here, I removed all bonuses to the Damage Base. Even more, Damage Base is no longer a mechanic surfaced to the players, it’s a design tool I used to balance and compare similar Moves. No abilities or features alter Damage Base anymore and the Moves now only list the number of dice the player should roll, if any.


Injury System

It's an additional health point system. If any Pokemon were to accrue enough injuries, independently of their HP, they faint. Injuries heal at a different rate than HP and are acquired by meeting different conditions, like receiving heavy blows.

Injuries reduce the pokemon’s maximum HP. So a pokemon with healing abilities would eventually get so many injuries that it’d make it impossible for them to heal up enough to sustain the damage they are taking. So it is sort of a countermeasure to recovery strategies.


I took a different approach. I removed injuries and tried to handle recovery strategies by limiting them through the Moves Frequency mechanic.


Moves have a Frequency, they can be used a number of times at different intervals. At-will can be used freely, scene moves can be used a number of times per fight and daily moves a number of times per day. Healing moves fall generally under the daily category, because they influence HP, which is a resource restored by resting.


So I made the compromise of removing the Injury system but, in consequence, I had to be extra careful with the number of uses allowed healing moves.


Situational vs Significant, Volatile vs Static

There are a lot of sources for Bonuses in PTU. Moves, equipment, consumables, food, abilities, features, edges. And… I think that’s it, but I might be missing a few. But the crunchy part is not the number of sources, it’s the fact that they may come and go or that they might be active due to certain criteria being met. And multiple of them could be active or inactive at the same time, having to tweak the numbers every turn.

And there is this concept of reducing the amount of headspace required by the players. In this case, it means that the player is trying to keep track of all of this situational bonuses and penalties and trying to remember when they need to apply and when they expire. This takes up space in their minds, which might be fun for some players, but it is a consequence of crunchy mechanics.

If the bonuses were only static (which many but not all are), the game would need some prep-work but most of the playtime would be just referencing the numbers of the sheets and rolling. But the bonuses change, which means a lot of on-the-fly calculations.

So to reduce the crunchiness here, I took a few pages from Dungeons & Dragons 5th edition. Bonuses are now only static or significant (and unique). Static bonuses make you work up-front, so they are good for this purpose. The other method is making them significant (and unique). You can increase significance by turning the bonus number into an action, like rolling a die. The Advantage and Disadvantage mechanic is an example of significant bonuses in dungeons and dragons.

Advantage and Disadvantage
Characters rolling with either Advantage and Disadvantage roll two d20 when taking the affected action. If they have advantage, they pick the highest roll of the two. If they have Disadvantage, they pick the lowest.


Uniqueness is a compromise. This means that you choose that players cannot get more than one bonus to the same action because there isn’t one. This is the only one. An example of this is the bless spell in Dungeons and Dragons.

Bless is a volatile bonus, I lasts only one minute or until the caster breaks their concentration. And it reads:

You bless up to three creatures of your choice within range. Whenever a target makes an attack roll or a saving throw before the spell ends, the target can roll a d4 and add the number rolled to the attack roll or saving throw.


This means that when they roll or try to save, they roll a d20 + an additional d4. This bonus is not only unique because it’s the only bonus (that I‘m aware of) that applies to attack rolls, it’s also significant, because the player is rolling an additional die. And it’s not a complete subsystem like Advantage, it’s a relatively small bonus, and if there were multiple of these, players would have to remember to roll for all of them and keep track of them at the same time (the Concentration mechanic also helps here). But because this is the only one, people roll a d20, add the static modifier and ask, do we have bless? And whatever the answer, they move on to advantage.

So what did this mean for my version of PTU? It meant getting rid of volatile and situational bonuses and replacing them with significant and unique ones. Did I achieve that? Partially. I added an advantage system and that helped to get rid of the attack bonuses, but since I was trying to keep the amount of content the same, it meant that it was hard to ensure uniqueness, and I failed at that. Taking one step forward would be by cutting content. There are a lot of Moves and Abilities, and they add uniqueness to the pokemon themselves, but also add complexity to the game.


Combat Stages, a Volatile System

Here is another compromise I had to make. One I'm not entirely satisfied with.


Combat stages is a core mechanic in the pokemon games, and PTU has most, if not all, of those in their rules, plus some extras (like injuries). They are not 1 to 1 translations, they are adaptations and pretty good ones at that. Combat stages in the pokemon games are multipliers to pokemon stats. Multiplication is a basic operation, but not the most basic. So I replaced the multiplication part with addition.


But combat stages vary throughout a battle. They are affected by Moves and abilities and features. They are volatile and, as established, crunchy. So I should remove it, right?

Well, here is the issue, Combat Stages is too much of a core mechanic of pokemon games. One solution was to turn the most important buff Moves into unique and iconic Buffs. Like Haste or Barkskin in D&D. But this meant cuttin other moves, which is something I didn’t want to do.

So Combat Stages stayed. I'm pretty sure they are the only volatile bonus remaining (except for some abilities I failed to translate), and they are a core mechanic of the game, but still, it takes up headspace, and makes the game a bit more crunchy.


Complex Skills

Some RPGs have skills, they represent your capabilities outside of combat. Are you good at bartering? How much do you know about the world? If you try to be charming, what are your chances of actually succeeding? All of these are also represented in PTU.

Skills had some issues that increased the game’s complexity and crunchiness. Skills, here, affected many things. And example would be that to calculate how many spaces a character could move on the board they had to average their athletics and acrobatics skills together. Skills also influenced your carry capacity, which should be ok, but the thing that affected crunchiness the most was Edges.

Edges are small, unique and flavorful abilities used to spice up a character. Most Edges had an influence over combat, or you could spend edges to level up a skill on a 1-to-1 basis. A player character acquired 4 edges at level 1 and 1 every other level thereafter. This meant that players had to make a few choices at the first level only. And some other while leveling up. But there were two issues here. One is that they weren’t really impactful or powerful. This is a common pattern in crunchier systems, where characters and their power level are actually sums of smaller choices and bonuses.

The other issue laid with the other choice system. Features. On the levels you didn’t gain an edge, you earned a Feature. Features were more powerful, approximately twice as much, than an Edge. But to qualify for a Feature you generally had to meet some requirements. Those requirements came, generally in the form of Skills, and some requirements where pretty tight. This meant that you had to plan your skills ahead of time if you wanted to hit the Features you wanted, and that took some time.


So how to solve this? Cut the edges. Players get a skill point every two levels and I buffed some iconic Edges and turned them into Features. Features were also grouped by classes and you could take them in any order. I removed that as well, classes are now more linear and allow fewer choices but the process is more streamlined (classes sort of had a dependency path already so it wasn’t that hard).


Other changes

Attack AC

Simplifying, all moves had a penalty to their attack roll. It was, generally, -2. So I got rid of the penalty. I thought about increasing defense by +2 for everyone, but in the end I just ditched it. Moves with higher penalties now roll with Disadvantage (roll twice, pick worse).


Tutor Points

Pokemon had these points that allowed them to learn extra moves and abilities outside of the ones they could learn by leveling up. I removed them. These points were a throttle to avoid players teaching too many of these to their pokemon, but there were already some other throttlers in place, in addition to these points, so I had to be careful, but I thought I could do without them.

Ability Points

Another throttler. This time it’s for player characters. Some abilities were too powerful and thus had to be limited and had their usage weighed against other abilities. I decided to balance them via the Frequency mechanic instead.

The bulk of the work

It was going over every Feature, Ability and Move in the game. Adapt it and rework it if necessary. I still keep the ones that I couldn’t make work in my notes. If you want to look for them, they are volatile.

3 Pokemon per team

This is more of a house rule, but tracking all 6 pokemon per player might be too much, so in my playgroup we limit it.


Want to play it?

If you want to check out my version of PTU, there is a link below for it as well as for the companion spreadsheet. Both are required. In the document you will find the core rules and Features and in the spreadsheet there’s the list of all items, abilities and moves. If you want to play, it’s better if you are familiar with the original and willing to make your own changes to these rules, since, as I mentioned above, this wasn't playtested.

The only dependency of my ruleset to the older ruleset is the Pokedex PDF which you will have to download from the original rules package (also linked below). Instructions on how to use it are in the Pokemon Progression section of my document.

Summary

It was a long but fun project. It is not complete, but it is ready to playtest. Sadly I never got to playtest it with my group. Someday I might, or, most likely, I'll build a softer system from the ground up, making compromises on my own terms.


If you read this far, thank you for reading. I hope this has been insightful for learning about my creative process.
